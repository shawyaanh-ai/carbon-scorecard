What Is Additionality?

A carbon credit is "additional" if the emissions reduction would not have happened without the revenue from selling that credit. This is the "but-for" test: but for this funding, would the forest still be standing?

The analogy: Paying someone $10 not to eat a cake they weren't going to eat anyway. The cake was never at risk , your payment bought nothing real.

Researchers at Berkeley, Oxford, and CarbonPlan found 85% of offsets sold today are not additional , meaning buyers paid for emissions reductions that would have occurred regardless.

What Are Phantom Credits?

Phantom credits are issued against a deliberately inflated baseline , the project developer overstates how much deforestation would have occurred without the project, then claims credit for "preventing" deforestation that was never going to happen.

For Kariba in Zimbabwe, researchers found the project received 30× more credits than it should have. South Pole's own satellite analysis found 27 million of Kariba's credits were fabricated.

When corporations "offset" their emissions with phantom credits, their net-zero claims are mathematically false.

Environmental Justice: The Human Cost of Fraud

Kariba, Zimbabwe: The Kariba REDD+ project generated approximately €100 million in carbon credit revenue between 2011 and 2023. A fraction reached the villages in Nyaminyami, Binga, Mbire, and Hurungwe , the four districts whose communal woodlands underwrote those credits. Researchers documented "functional displacement": communities lost access to land for agriculture, firewood, and traditional use without receiving the promised benefits. The money flowed through a Guernsey-registered company, the trail deliberately obscured. Steve Wentzel, the entrepreneur behind the project, later admitted to "illegal" money-hiding practices.

This is not an edge case. A 2024 review found that 10 of 16 UN-REDD countries violated Free Prior Informed Consent (FPIC) requirements , the legal obligation to seek indigenous community consent before starting a project. In Cambodia, FPIC meetings for a Verra-certified project began 31 months after operations started.

Due Diligence

What Should an Investor
Actually Look For?

Post-2023 institutional buyers have shifted from volume-based purchasing to integrity-based screening. Here is the framework that separates high-confidence buys from reputational landmines.

Green Flags

ICVCM CCP-Label Seek credits explicitly carrying the CCP label , not just CCP-Eligible registries, but the label applied to specific approved methodologies. As of 2026, this is the highest credibility signal in the market.

Independent Third-Party Verifier with No Financial Relationship to Developer The auditor must not be selected or paid directly by the project developer. Look for registry-assigned verification.

Documented FPIC and Community Benefit-Sharing Project documentation should include signed FPIC agreements, benefit-sharing ledgers, and third-party community impact assessments , not developer self-reports.

Conservative Baseline Methodology Seek projects using conservative, peer-reviewed deforestation models , not developer-estimated baselines. BeZero Carbon and Sylvera ratings are useful third-party screens.

Buffer Pool Disclosure Registry should maintain a transparent, independently managed buffer pool equal to at least 10,20% of issued credits to cover reversals. ACR's buffer pool methodology is a reference standard.

Red Flags

REDD+ Credits Without Post-2023 Re-Verification Any REDD+ credit issued before mid-2023 and not re-verified under updated methodologies should be treated as suspect. The Guardian investigation specifically targeted this cohort.

Developer-Controlled Governance Structure Projects where the developer controls community consultation, benefit distribution, and verification oversight simultaneously have structural conflicts of interest. Kariba is the defining case study.

Renewable Energy Credits in Mature Markets Solar and wind additionality in grid-connected markets with >5% renewable penetration is almost impossible to demonstrate. Gold Standard restricted these credits from India in 2020 for exactly this reason.

Offshore Corporate Structure with Opaque Revenue Flows Project developers incorporated in tax havens (Cayman, Guernsey, BVI) with no transparent community revenue ledger is a documented pattern in fraudulent REDD+ projects.

No BeZero/Sylvera Rating or "D" Rating BeZero Carbon and Sylvera provide independent credit quality ratings. A "D" rating (as issued for Kariba) or absence of rating from a major project should trigger heightened due diligence.

Methodology & Limitations

A scoring framework is only as credible as its underlying logic and honesty about what it cannot do.

Scoring Design Rationale

Each of the 6 criteria is scored 0,10 and weighted equally (60 points total). Equal weighting is a deliberate choice: no single criterion is sufficient on its own, and the ICVCM CCPs treat additionality, permanence, and community impact as co-equal pillars. A project that scores perfectly on additionality but fails on community impact should not receive a high overall rating , which is precisely what equal weighting achieves.

Scores are derived from primary source research: registry documentation, peer-reviewed studies, investigative journalism, third-party rating agency assessments (BeZero, Sylvera), and field reporting from Human Rights Watch, REDD Monitor, and Carbon Brief.

ICVCM CCP Alignment

The ICVCM's 10 Core Carbon Principles (released March 2023) are the first global governance standard for voluntary carbon markets with genuine enforcement mechanisms. Each scoring criterion maps directly to one or more CCPs:

  • Additionality → CCP #5
  • Permanence → CCP #6
  • Verification Quality → CCP #3 (Third-Party Validation)
  • Registry Credibility → CCP #1 (Effective Governance)
  • Community Impact → CCP #9 (Sustainable Development)
  • Price Stability → Market signal, no direct CCP mapping

Honest Limitations

This scorecard has real constraints that any buyer should understand:

Subjectivity Scores involve qualitative judgment. Two analysts reviewing the same project may reach different conclusions. This framework should be a starting point, not a final verdict. Static Data Carbon markets move fast. Prices, registry standings, and project statuses change. This analysis reflects the state of information as of early 2026. Portfolio Gap Scoring 10 credits is illustrative. A real institutional buyer needs portfolio-level scoring across hundreds of credits with automated data feeds. Price Proxy Price stability is used as a proxy for market confidence but does not directly measure environmental integrity.
About This Project

Built at UC Berkeley

Consult Your Community (CYC) , Consulting Capstone 2026

This tool was built as a capstone consulting project for Consult Your Community (CYC) at UC Berkeley , a student-run consulting organization that partners with nonprofits, community organizations, and socially-minded enterprises.

The voluntary carbon market sits at the intersection of climate finance, indigenous rights, and corporate accountability. When it works, it channels billions of dollars toward genuine emissions reductions in forests that would otherwise be cleared. When it fails , as it spectacularly did in 2023 , it provides cover for polluters, launders corporate greenwashing, and in documented cases, directly harms the indigenous communities it claims to protect.

This framework exists because the information asymmetry between credit sellers and institutional buyers is structural. Project developers control their own auditors, set their own baselines, and control community benefit distribution. The 2023 collapse was not a market failure in the traditional sense , it was a governance failure enabled by insufficient scrutiny tools available to buyers.

The environmental justice dimension cannot be separated from the financial integrity question. The communities whose land underpins REDD+ credits , in Zimbabwe, DRC Congo, Indonesia, Peru , bear the real-world costs of fraudulent accounting in ways that never appear in a credit's price history.
